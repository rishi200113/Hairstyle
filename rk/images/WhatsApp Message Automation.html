The "bathtub curve" is a graphical representation often used in reliability engineering to illustrate the failure rate of components or systems over their lifespan. It's called a "bathtub" curve because when plotted on a graph, it roughly resembles the shape of a bathtub.

The curve typically consists of three periods:

  1. Early Life Failures (Infant Mortality): At the beginning of a component's life, there is a higher probability of failures due to manufacturing defects, handling issues, or other factors related to the initial installation or usage. This phase is characterized by a relatively high failure rate. Components that fail in this phase are often referred to as experiencing "infant mortality."

  2. Useful Life (Random Failures): During the useful life of the component, the failure rate remains relatively low and constant. This phase represents the period where the component operates normally without significant failures. The failure rate is relatively stable during this time, reflecting random failures that can occur due to wear and tear, environmental stresses, or other factors.

  3. Wear-out Failures: As the component approaches the end of its useful life, the failure rate begins to increase gradually. This phase is characterized by an increasing failure rate due to aging, wear, or degradation of the component's materials and mechanisms. Components eventually reach a point where the failure rate becomes unacceptably high, leading to functional failure or retirement from service.

The bathtub curve concept is essential in reliability engineering as it helps engineers understand and predict the failure behavior of components or systems over time. By identifying the different failure modes and their associated failure rates, engineers can develop strategies to mitigate failures, improve reliability, and extend the lifespan of products or systems.
